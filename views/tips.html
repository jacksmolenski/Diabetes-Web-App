I have lived with diabetes since I was 15 months old, and feel that I have the expertise to share tips. These are things I have done to keep a steady A1C in the 6-7 range as a Type 1 Diabetic. I prioritize my health, but I also want to make sure I spend as little time as possible treating myself. I currently use a T-Slim insulin pump paired with a Dexcom G6 Continuous Glucose Monitor.

Health Tips

Mental Health

I can say that with having diabetes my entire life, I have never felt insecure because of my diabetes. But, I know from hearing from others that sometimes that is not the case. To start, I can say I have been made fun of for my diabetes. On rare occasions it has happened, but so much more than the negativity I have received for it, I have received so much love, and part of that is because of how I carry myself living with diabetes. Let me suggest these few things for the mental side of things

  • Joke about your diabetes. From a young age I would joke about it myself with my friends. Acknowledging it sucks is a huge help, but joking about it seems to really help me. It can be pretty great to have a good laugh with your friends or family about it. Also joking about it makes it less of a vulnerability for yourself, because if you're able to laugh about it, you're less likely to be made fun of for it.
  • Use it to your advantage. As a child, I would use my diabetes as an excuse to get out of class, or out of things I didn't want to do. I can't say this excuse is morally correct, but, I feel as if I have the right to use it as an excuse. I could also eat candy or snacks in class when other kids couldn't, and that felt like a reward to me. And on top of that, using it to your advantage helps to develop a positive mindset related to diabetes, which greatly helped me because I really don't have negative association with my diabetes.
  • Realize it could be worse. I remind myself so much I could have it far worse. I have to take a few minutes out of my day to treat myself. Maybe on average 5-20 minutes a day. It truly is not that big of a deal, and many people have it far worse with other diseases or life situations. All things considered, diabetes is not the hardest thing to treat / live with.

Physical Health

  • Get outside! This works for me best best I love nature, I enjoy being outdoors, and I love to play sports. I like fishing, hiking, football, soccer, basketball, or really anything outside. This keeps you in shape, is a great way to keep your blood sugar down. Also ties into the mental health side because being outside is proven to raise your mood!
  • Go to the gym. This is just a simple way to stay physically active, it helps with your metabolism, and also is helpful to me in the sense of keeping my blood sugar lower due to the physical activity.
  • Eating healthy or just a consistent list of food. Eating healthy allows for a more consistent blood sugar, and it doesn't spike your blood sugar like sweets or junk food can. Or, if you do eat sweets, eat them in a consistent manner. The junk food I enjoy, I have a mental note of how much insulin I need so I can make supe my blood sugar doesn't spike. I hate feeling high, and I also hate having my blood sugar alarms go off. So I must emphasize this advice.

Dosing and Treating Tips

Keep in mind, this is based off of the T-Slim pump and Dexcom working together for my results. Also, my doctors have mixed feelings about these tips, but, they work and the results show it. So ultimately, my doctors tell me to keep doing what I am doing.

Insulin Dosage

  • Instead of counting my carbs for every meal I eat, I keep track in my mind roughly how many units of insulin I would need for that meal. Given, this has taken effort to keep a mental note of the estimate of insulin I need for a meal, but in the long run it saves me time. With todays technology of the Dexcom and T-Slim working together, the doses don't have to be perfect, the pump does a great job adjusting for these estimations not being perfect. I find this tip very helpful because it can be so annoying to carb count, and I have little desire to put that much effort into my diabetes. Also, make sure you dose before the meal, not during or after! I have found this saves me from so many high blood sugars!
  • Secondly, for high blood sugar corrections, I find it is super helpful to have an understanding in your mind how much insulin is needed when your blood sugar is high. Call me crazy, but I will emphasize that I want to spend as little time as possible treating my diabetes. Instead of entering my blood sugar into the pump and letting it calculate the dose, I have a mental note of how much insulin would be needed to bring my blood sugar down to a normal range. Is this dumb? Maybe, but in the grand scheme of things, it saves me a few seconds and some mental effort for each correction, and that brings me peace of mind and makes my diabetes easier to manage. Along with that, it works consistently. If you know the dosage, I say why not take the short cut to giving yourself this insulin!

Low Blood Sugar Treatments

  • I cannot act like I haven't fallen victim to this before, but please, do not over treat your lows. Being low, then taking in a lot of carbs and or sugar, which then sends you high, is not a fun roller coaster to be on. No matter how low you feel, make sure you treat your low with the amount suggested by your doctor. I think the standard is around 15 grams of carbs, so please do this. It will save you so much time in the long run.
  • This one is a bit of a gamble, but also very interesting. I have discovered that for some of my less severe lows, but the ones where I feel my heart start to beat faster, there is an interesting way I have been able to treat them. That way is by focusing on slowing my heart rate down. This is because a slower heart rate requires less sugar to enter the stream, and it can make your blood sugar stablize/ raise again.
